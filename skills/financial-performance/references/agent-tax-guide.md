## 2. Self-Employment Tax Calculation

### Step-by-Step Walkthrough

Self-employment tax applies to all net earnings from self-employment (Schedule C income for sole proprietors and single-member LLCs).

**Step 1: Determine Net SE Earnings**
- Start with Schedule C net profit (gross income minus business deductions).
- Multiply by 92.35% (0.9235). This adjustment mirrors the fact that employees only pay FICA on wages after the employer's share is excluded.

**Step 2: Calculate Social Security Tax**
- Rate: 12.4%
- Wage base for 2025: $176,100
- If net SE earnings (after 92.35% adjustment) exceed $176,100, Social Security tax caps at $176,100 x 12.4% = $21,836.40.

**Step 3: Calculate Medicare Tax**
- Rate: 2.9% on all net SE earnings (no cap)
- Additional Medicare Tax: 0.9% on SE income exceeding $200,000 (single) or $250,000 (MFJ)

**Step 4: Total SE Tax**
- Social Security portion + Medicare portion = Total SE tax
- Maximum rate: 15.3% on first $176,100, then 2.9% (or 3.8%) above that

**Step 5: Deductible Half**
- 50% of SE tax is deductible on Form 1040 (above the line, reduces AGI)
- This deduction reduces income tax but does NOT reduce SE tax itself

### Example Calculation: $110,000 Net Business Income

| Step | Calculation | Amount |
|------|------------|--------|
| Net business income | Schedule C | $110,000 |
| SE earnings (x 0.9235) | $110,000 x 0.9235 | $101,585 |
| Social Security tax | $101,585 x 12.4% | $12,597 |
| Medicare tax | $101,585 x 2.9% | $2,946 |
| **Total SE tax** | $12,597 + $2,946 | **$15,543** |
| Deductible half | $15,543 x 50% | $7,772 |

Effective SE tax rate on the original $110,000: 14.1%.

### Example Calculation: $300,000 Net Business Income

| Step | Calculation | Amount |
|------|------------|--------|
| Net business income | Schedule C | $300,000 |
| SE earnings (x 0.9235) | $300,000 x 0.9235 | $277,050 |
| Social Security tax | $176,100 x 12.4% (capped) | $21,836 |
| Medicare tax | $277,050 x 2.9% | $8,034 |
| Additional Medicare (>$200K) | $77,050 x 0.9% | $693 |
| **Total SE tax** | $21,836 + $8,034 + $693 | **$30,563** |
| Deductible half | $30,563 x 50% | $15,282 |

Effective SE tax rate on the original $300,000: 10.2% (rate drops at higher incomes due to SS cap).

## 3. Business Entity Comparison

### Sole Proprietorship vs. LLC vs. S-Corp

| Factor | Sole Proprietorship | Single-Member LLC | S-Corp (LLC or Corp electing S status) |
|--------|--------------------|--------------------|----------------------------------------|
| **Formation** | None required | State filing ($50-$500) | State filing + IRS Form 2553 |
| **Liability Protection** | None | Yes (with proper maintenance) | Yes |
| **Tax Return** | Schedule C on personal 1040 | Schedule C on personal 1040 (default) | Form 1120-S + K-1 to personal 1040 |
| **SE Tax Treatment** | 15.3% on all net income | 15.3% on all net income (same as sole prop) | 15.3% on salary ONLY; distributions exempt |
| **Compliance Cost** | Minimal | $0-$800/year (state fees) | $2,000-$4,000/year (payroll + tax return + state fees) |
| **Payroll Required** | No | No | Yes -- must pay reasonable salary |
| **Retirement Plan Access** | SEP-IRA, Solo 401(k) | SEP-IRA, Solo 401(k) | SEP-IRA, Solo 401(k) (based on W-2 wages) |
| **Audit Risk Factor** | Standard | Standard | Reasonable salary scrutiny |
| **When to Consider** | Default starting point | Immediately (liability protection) | Net income consistently above $60K-$80K |

### S-Corp Reasonable Salary Guidelines for RE Agents

The IRS requires S-Corp owners to pay themselves a "reasonable salary" for services performed. There is no fixed percentage, but the following factors are considered:

- Training and experience of the agent
- Duties and responsibilities
- Time and effort devoted to the business
- Comparable salaries for similar roles in the market
- Production level and history

**General guidelines used by RE CPAs:**

| Net Business Income | Typical Reasonable Salary Range | Salary as % of Net |
|---------------------|--------------------------------|---------------------|
| $60K-$80K | $40K-$50K | 60-65% |
| $80K-$120K | $50K-$65K | 50-60% |
| $120K-$200K | $60K-$90K | 45-55% |
| $200K-$400K | $80K-$120K | 30-40% |
| $400K+ | $100K-$150K | 25-35% |

**Warning:** Setting salary too low is the primary audit trigger for S-Corp RE agents. Agents who pay themselves $30K on $200K net income are inviting IRS scrutiny.

### When to Switch: Decision Framework

| Current Situation | Recommendation |
|------------------|----------------|
| Year 1, under $40K net | Stay sole prop; form LLC for liability protection |
| Year 2-3, $40K-$60K net | LLC in place; evaluate S-Corp annually |
| Consistent $60K-$80K+ net | Model S-Corp savings vs. compliance costs with CPA |
| $100K+ net and growing | S-Corp election likely beneficial -- act before March 15 of the tax year |
| Inconsistent income (good year/bad year) | Wait until income is consistently above threshold; S-Corp in a low-income year adds cost without savings |

**S-Corp Election Deadline:** File Form 2553 by March 15 of the tax year for the election to be effective for that year. Late elections may be accepted with reasonable cause.

## 4. Quarterly Estimated Tax Deadlines & Safe Harbor

### Payment Schedule

| Quarter | Income Period | Due Date | IRS Form |
|---------|--------------|----------|----------|
| Q1 | January 1 - March 31 | April 15 | 1040-ES |
| Q2 | April 1 - May 31 | June 15 | 1040-ES |
| Q3 | June 1 - August 31 | September 15 | 1040-ES |
| Q4 | September 1 - December 31 | January 15 (next year) | 1040-ES |

**Note:** If the due date falls on a weekend or holiday, the deadline moves to the next business day.

### Safe Harbor Rules (Avoiding Underpayment Penalties)

You will NOT owe an underpayment penalty if you meet any of these criteria:

| Method | Requirement | Best For |
|--------|-------------|----------|
| **Prior Year Safe Harbor** | Pay 100% of prior year's total tax liability in four equal quarterly payments | Agents with growing income (pay based on last year's lower amount) |
| **Prior Year Safe Harbor (High Income)** | Pay 110% of prior year tax if AGI exceeded $150K ($75K if MFS) | High-earning agents whose income varies |
| **Current Year Method** | Pay 90% of current year's tax liability | Agents with decreasing income |
| **No Penalty Threshold** | Total tax owed is less than $1,000 after credits and withholding | First-year agents or very low income |

### Recommended Approach for RE Agents

**Year 1:** Difficult to estimate. Set aside 30% of every commission check. Make quarterly payments based on projected income. Adjust Q3 and Q4 payments as actual income becomes clearer.

**Year 2+:** Use the prior-year safe harbor method. Take last year's total tax (line 24 of Form 1040), divide by 4, pay that amount each quarter. If AGI exceeded $150K, use 110% of prior year tax divided by 4.

**Underpayment Penalty Rate:** Variable, based on federal short-term rate + 3 percentage points. As of early 2025, approximately 7-8%. Compounded daily. Not trivial.

### Commonly Missed Deductions

1. **Cell phone (business %):** If the phone is used 70% for business, 70% of the monthly cost is deductible.
2. **Internet (business %):** Same proportional deduction applies.
3. **Continuing education:** Including travel to CE courses and conferences.
4. **Client event costs:** Holiday parties, client appreciation events, homebuyer seminars.
5. **Lockbox and key fees:** Supra or other lockbox system fees.
6. **Professional wardrobe:** Generally NOT deductible (IRS considers business attire suitable for everyday wear). Exception: branded uniforms or specialized clothing.
7. **MLS fines or penalties:** Deductible as a business expense (they are a cost of doing business, even if unpleasant).

## 6. Vehicle Deduction Comparison

### Standard Mileage Rate vs. Actual Expenses

| Factor | Standard Mileage Rate | Actual Expense Method |
|--------|----------------------|----------------------|
| **2025 Rate** | $0.70 per business mile | N/A (actual costs) |
| **What It Covers** | Gas, insurance, maintenance, depreciation, registration | Must track each actual cost individually |
| **Recordkeeping** | Mileage log (date, destination, purpose, miles) | Mileage log PLUS all receipts, insurance bills, maintenance records |
| **Simplicity** | High | Low |
| **Lease vs. Own** | Can use for either (with limitations on leased vehicles) | Can use for either |
| **First-Year Requirement** | Must choose mileage in the first year the vehicle is used for business to keep the option open | Can switch to mileage in future years if mileage was used first year |

### When Each Method Is Better

| Situation | Better Method | Why |
|-----------|--------------|-----|
| Drive 15,000+ business miles/year in a fuel-efficient car | Standard Mileage | High miles x $0.70 usually exceeds actual costs for economical vehicles |
| Drive a luxury/expensive vehicle with high depreciation | Actual Expenses | Depreciation on a $60K+ vehicle often exceeds mileage rate benefit |
| Don't want complex recordkeeping | Standard Mileage | Only need to track miles, not all expenses |
| New/expensive vehicle with high insurance and payments | Actual Expenses | Depreciation + high insurance + financing interest can exceed mileage |
| Older paid-off vehicle with low costs | Standard Mileage | Low actual costs make mileage rate the winner |

### Example Comparison: 18,000 Business Miles

| Method | Calculation | Deduction |
|--------|------------|-----------|
| **Standard Mileage** | 18,000 x $0.70 | $12,600 |
| **Actual Expenses** | Gas ($4,800) + Insurance ($1,800) + Maintenance ($1,200) + Depreciation ($4,000) + Registration ($400) = $12,200 x 75% business use | $9,150 |

In this example, the standard mileage rate produces a larger deduction by $3,450.

### Critical Recordkeeping Requirements

The IRS can disallow the ENTIRE vehicle deduction if the mileage log is not contemporaneous (recorded at or near the time of the trip). Requirements:

- Date of each trip
- Destination (address or description)
- Business purpose
- Odometer reading at start and end (or trip miles)
- Total miles for the year (business + personal)

**Recommended:** Use a mileage tracking app (MileIQ, Everlance, Stride) that records trips automatically via GPS. Manual logs are acceptable but harder to maintain consistently.

## 7. Home Office Deduction

### Eligibility Requirements

The space must be used **regularly and exclusively** for business. "Exclusive use" means the area is used ONLY for business -- a kitchen table used for both meals and paperwork does not qualify. A dedicated room or clearly defined area of a room qualifies.

**Exception for RE agents:** Real estate agents who have no other fixed office location can deduct home office expenses even if the space is also used for other purposes, as long as it is used regularly for administrative or management activities. This is a specific statutory exception under IRC Section 280A(c)(2).

### Simplified Method vs. Actual Method

| Factor | Simplified Method | Actual Method |
|--------|------------------|---------------|
| **Calculation** | $5 per square foot | Actual expenses x business % |
| **Maximum** | 300 sq ft = $1,500/year | No maximum |
| **Expenses Tracked** | None (flat rate) | Mortgage interest/rent, insurance, utilities, repairs, depreciation |
| **Recordkeeping** | Measure the room | Track all home expenses + calculate business percentage |
| **Depreciation** | Not claimed (no recapture on sale) | Claimed (potential recapture on home sale) |
| **Carryover** | None (use it or lose it each year) | Excess can carry forward |

### When Each Method Is Better

| Situation | Better Method | Typical Deduction |
|-----------|--------------|-------------------|
| Small dedicated space (<300 sq ft), simple recordkeeping preferred | Simplified | Up to $1,500 |
| Large dedicated space (300+ sq ft) in expensive home | Actual | $3,000-$8,000+ |
| Renter with high rent payments | Actual | Proportional rent often exceeds $1,500 |
| Homeowner concerned about depreciation recapture on future home sale | Simplified | Avoids recapture issue entirely |

### Business Percentage Calculation (Actual Method)

**Method 1: Square footage**
Business % = Square footage of office / Total square footage of home

**Method 2: Room count**
Business % = Number of rooms used for business / Total rooms in home

Example: 200 sq ft office in a 2,000 sq ft home = 10% business use.
Total home expenses: $30,000 (mortgage interest $15K + property tax $5K + insurance $2K + utilities $4K + maintenance $2K + depreciation $2K).
Deduction: $30,000 x 10% = $3,000.

## 8. Retirement Plan Comparison

### Options for Self-Employed RE Agents (2025 Limits)

| Plan | Employee Contribution | Employer Contribution | Total Maximum | Catch-Up (50+) | Roth Option | Income Limit |
|------|----------------------|----------------------|---------------|-----------------|-------------|--------------|
| **SEP-IRA** | N/A | Up to 25% of net SE income (after SE tax deduction) | $70,000 | None | No | None |
| **Solo 401(k)** | Up to $23,500 | Up to 25% of net SE income (after SE tax deduction) | $70,000 | +$7,500 (or +$11,250 ages 60-63) | Yes (employee portion) | None |
| **Traditional IRA** | Up to $7,000 | N/A | $7,000 | +$1,000 | No | Deductibility phases out with high AGI if covered by retirement plan |
| **Roth IRA** | Up to $7,000 | N/A | $7,000 | +$1,000 | Yes (entire contribution) | $150K-$165K MAGI (single), $236K-$246K (MFJ) |

### Plan Comparison Detail

**SEP-IRA (Simplified Employee Pension)**
- **Pros:** Easy to set up, no annual filing, flexible contributions (can vary year to year), high contribution limits.
- **Cons:** No employee (elective deferral) contributions -- only employer contributions. No Roth option. No catch-up contributions. If you have W-2 employees, must contribute the same percentage for them.
- **Best for:** Solo agents under $100K net who want simplicity and flexibility.

**Solo 401(k) (Individual 401(k))**
- **Pros:** Highest possible contribution limits (employee + employer). Roth option available. Loan provision available. Catch-up contributions for age 50+.
- **Cons:** More complex setup. Annual Form 5500-EZ filing required if plan assets exceed $250K. Cannot have non-owner W-2 employees (except spouse).
- **Best for:** Agents earning $100K+ net who want to maximize retirement savings and/or want Roth contributions. The dual employee + employer contribution structure allows much higher savings at moderate income levels compared to SEP-IRA.

**Traditional IRA**
- **Pros:** Simple, universally available, tax-deductible contributions.
- **Cons:** Low $7,000 limit. Deductibility phases out if covered by an employer plan (Solo 401(k) or SEP-IRA).
- **Best for:** Supplement to SEP-IRA or Solo 401(k), not a primary vehicle.

**Roth IRA**
- **Pros:** Tax-free growth and withdrawals in retirement. No RMDs. Contributions (not earnings) can be withdrawn anytime penalty-free.
- **Cons:** Low $7,000 limit. Income limits apply. Contributions are not tax-deductible.
- **Best for:** Younger agents expecting higher future income. Agents who want tax diversification in retirement.

### Example: Maximizing Contributions at Different Income Levels

| Net SE Income | SEP-IRA Max | Solo 401(k) Max | Advantage |
|---------------|-------------|-----------------|-----------|
| $50,000 | $9,296 | $23,500 + $9,296 = $32,796 | Solo 401(k) wins by $23,500 |
| $100,000 | $18,587 | $23,500 + $18,587 = $42,087 | Solo 401(k) wins by $23,500 |
| $200,000 | $37,175 | $23,500 + $37,175 = $60,675 | Solo 401(k) wins by $23,500 |
| $280,000+ | $70,000 (cap) | $70,000 (cap) | Same at cap |

**Note:** The employer contribution percentage for Solo 401(k) and SEP-IRA is calculated on net SE income AFTER the deduction for half of SE tax. The actual effective percentage on gross net income is approximately 18.6%, not 25%.

## 9. Health Insurance Deduction

### Self-Employed Health Insurance Deduction

RE agents who are self-employed (sole prop, LLC, or S-Corp shareholder owning >2%) can deduct health insurance premiums for themselves, their spouse, and dependents.

**Key Rules:**
- Deduction is taken on Form 1040 (above the line) -- reduces AGI.
- Cannot exceed net SE income from the business.
- Cannot be taken for any month where the agent was eligible for an employer-subsidized health plan (including through a spouse's employer).
- Includes medical, dental, vision, and qualifying long-term care premiums.
- For S-Corp owners: premiums must be paid by the S-Corp or reported as W-2 income, then deducted on the personal return.

### ACA Marketplace Considerations

Many RE agents purchase health insurance through the ACA marketplace (Healthcare.gov or state exchanges).

- Premium Tax Credit (subsidy) is based on household income relative to the Federal Poverty Level.
- MAGI between 100%-400% FPL qualifies for premium tax credits.
- Agents with variable income should estimate conservatively and reconcile at tax time.
- Self-employed health insurance deduction and Premium Tax Credit interact: you cannot deduct the subsidized portion of the premium.

### Typical Costs (2025 Estimates)

| Coverage Type | Monthly Premium Range (pre-subsidy) | Annual Cost |
|---------------|-------------------------------------|-------------|
| Individual (Silver plan, age 35-44) | $400-$700 | $4,800-$8,400 |
| Individual (Silver plan, age 45-54) | $550-$950 | $6,600-$11,400 |
| Family (Silver plan) | $1,200-$2,500 | $14,400-$30,000 |

Health insurance is often one of the largest single expenses for self-employed agents and a significant reason to evaluate S-Corp election (premiums become a deductible business expense through the S-Corp).

### Reciprocity Agreements (Key States)

Some states have reciprocity agreements where residents working across state lines only pay tax to their home state. Relevant for agents who work near state borders:

- **DC/MD/VA:** Full reciprocity among all three
- **NJ/PA:** Full reciprocity
- **IL/IA/KY/MI/WI:** Various reciprocity agreements with Illinois
- **IN/KY/MI/OH/PA/WI:** Various reciprocity agreements

**Note:** Reciprocity typically applies to W-2 wages, not self-employment income. RE agents operating (licensed and closing transactions) in multiple states may owe taxes in each state where they earn income, regardless of reciprocity. Consult a tax professional if you hold licenses in multiple states.

### State-Level Considerations for RE Agents

1. **Business entity fees vary dramatically by state.** California charges an $800 minimum franchise tax for LLCs annually regardless of income. Wyoming charges $60. This affects the LLC formation decision.
2. **Some states have additional business taxes.** Washington's B&O tax, Ohio's CAT (Commercial Activity Tax), Texas franchise tax -- these can apply even in "no income tax" states.
3. **State estimated tax payments have their own deadlines.** Many follow the federal schedule, but some states differ. Verify with your state's tax authority.
4. **Moving states mid-year** creates split-year filing requirements and can be complex. Plan the timing with a CPA.
5. **Working remotely does not automatically change your tax state.** Your tax home is where your principal place of business is located, which for RE agents is typically where they hold their license and conduct most transactions.
