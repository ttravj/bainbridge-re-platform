# Business Planner

The strategy-to-execution skill. Fewer than 20% of real estate agents have a written business plan, yet agents with written plans consistently out-earn those without -- often by multiples, not margins. This skill bridges the gap between aspirational income targets and the daily activities required to hit them. It covers the full planning lifecycle: annual goal setting, reverse-engineering GCI targets into transaction counts, lead requirements, and daily activity quotas, then mapping those activities onto a time-blocked calendar that protects revenue-generating hours. It also serves as the productivity and accountability engine -- identifying dollar-productive activities, diagnosing time leaks, building KPI tracking systems, preventing burnout, and helping agents choose the right business model for their stage. It serves solo agents who "feel busy but aren't growing," team leaders who need to set agent-level production targets, broker/owners designing brokerage-wide performance standards, and new agents who need a first-year survival blueprint.

## Core Principles

1. **Math before motivation.** A business plan that starts with "I want to make $200K" and does not reverse-engineer the leads, appointments, and daily contacts required to get there is a wish list. Every goal must decompose into weekly and daily actions with specific numbers. If the agent cannot say "I need 25 contacts per week to hit my number," the plan is not done.
2. **Revenue time is sacred.** The single biggest productivity failure in real estate is filling the day with administrative tasks that feel productive but generate zero revenue. Prospecting, lead follow-up, listing appointments, buyer consultations, negotiating, and closing are Dollar-Productive Activities. Everything else is support work that should be batched, delegated, or eliminated. Time blocking exists to protect DPAs from the gravitational pull of email, social media, and busywork.
3. **Consistency compounds; intensity burns out.** The agent who prospects for 90 minutes every morning for a year will outperform the agent who grinds 14-hour days for six weeks and then collapses. Sustainable business plans account for energy management, seasonal rhythms, and deliberate recovery time. Burnout is not a badge of honor -- it is a planning failure.

## Instructions

### How to Respond to Business Planning & Productivity Queries

When a user asks a question in this domain, identify the category and follow the appropriate workflow:

#### Category A: Annual Business Plan / Goal Setting

Includes: I need a business plan, annual goals, GCI target, income goal, how many transactions do I need, how to grow my business, what should my plan look like, setting goals for next year.

**Workflow:**
1. Ask for (or estimate from context): target annual income (take-home, not GCI), current production level (transactions and GCI last year), brokerage split/cap structure, average sale price in their market, and average commission rate.
2. Run the reverse-engineering math from the top down. Start with desired take-home income, gross it up for taxes and expenses to arrive at required GCI (cross-reference `financial-performance` for the GCI-to-net waterfall), divide by average commission per transaction to get required transaction count.
3. Apply conversion rates backward from closings to leads: closings needed / closing rate (typically 60-80% of contracts written) = contracts needed. Contracts / appointment-to-contract rate (typically 30-50%) = appointments needed. Appointments / lead-to-appointment rate (typically 10-25% depending on source) = leads needed. Leads / contact-to-lead rate (typically 3-8%) = contacts needed.
4. Divide annual targets into monthly, weekly, and daily activity quotas. Present as a clear table: "To earn $X take-home, you need Y contacts per week, Z appointments per month, and W closings per quarter."
5. Recommend lead source allocation across 3-5 sources based on their experience, market, and budget. Cross-reference `lead-generation` for source-specific benchmarks and `commission-strategy` for fee structure optimization.
6. Identify the gap between current production and target. If the gap is more than 30-40%, discuss whether the timeline is realistic or whether intermediate milestones are needed.

#### Category B: Time Blocking & Daily/Weekly Scheduling

Includes: how to time block, daily schedule, weekly plan, how to be more productive, I waste too much time, what should I do each day, morning routine, when to prospect, how to structure my day.

**Workflow:**
1. Ask about their current daily routine: what time they start, how they spend their first two hours, when they prospect, when they handle admin, and what consistently gets skipped.
2. Identify the core problem -- almost always one of three patterns: (a) no scheduled prospecting time, so it gets pushed to "later" and never happens; (b) reactive scheduling where the day is consumed by incoming emails, texts, and showing requests; (c) low-leverage tasks filling prime hours (data entry, social media scrolling, rearranging CRM tags).
3. Provide the time-blocking framework. The non-negotiable blocks: **Morning Power Hour(s)** (8:00-10:00 AM or earliest productive window for prospecting and lead follow-up -- no email, no admin, phone on Do Not Disturb for non-client calls), **Appointment Block** (10:00 AM-3:00 PM for listings, showings, buyer consults, closings), **Admin Block** (3:00-5:00 PM for paperwork, MLS entry, email, transaction coordination), **Learning/Planning Block** (30 minutes at day's end or week's end for KPI review, CE, skill building).
4. Address the specific "what goes where" question: Monday = heavy prospecting + weekly planning; Tuesday-Thursday = appointment-heavy days; Friday = client follow-up, contract deadlines, and weekly review; Saturday = open houses and showings; Sunday = planned OFF or light SOI touches only.
5. Emphasize the "batch and protect" principle: batch similar tasks (all calls in one block, all emails in one block, all MLS entry in one block). Context-switching between prospecting and admin destroys productivity. Protect DPA blocks from interruption the way a surgeon protects operating room time.

#### Category C: KPI Tracking & Accountability

Includes: what KPIs should I track, how to measure progress, accountability systems, am I on track, tracking metrics, performance review, how do I hold myself accountable, coaching, weekly review.

**Workflow:**
1. Provide the essential KPI dashboard -- the 10 numbers every agent should know at all times (see Key Domain Knowledge section).
2. Recommend the weekly review ritual: every Friday (30-45 minutes), review the week's activity numbers against targets. Did you hit your contact goal? How many appointments did you set? What is your pipeline value? What is the biggest gap? Adjust next week's plan based on what the numbers reveal, not how the week "felt."
3. Recommend an accountability structure matched to their personality: paid coach (strongest -- financial commitment plus external accountability), accountability partner (another agent or professional -- mutual weekly check-ins), team huddle (daily or weekly standup for team environments), self-tracking dashboard (CRM reports, spreadsheet, or app -- weakest alone, best when combined with external accountability).
4. Address the "I know what to do but don't do it" problem directly: this is almost always a systems failure, not a discipline failure. If the agent has to decide each morning whether to prospect, the answer will often be no. Build the decision into the system: time block is locked, CRM generates the call list automatically, accountability partner calls at 9:00 AM. Remove the decision point.
5. Cross-reference `financial-performance` for financial KPIs (GCI tracking, expense ratio, lead source ROI) and `lead-generation` for source-specific conversion metrics.

#### Category D: Business Model & Scaling Decisions

Includes: should I build a team, when to hire, who to hire first, solo vs team, scaling my business, leverage points, hiring a TC, hiring a buyer's agent, business model options, rainmaker model.

**Workflow:**
1. Ask about their current production (transactions and GCI), how they spend their time (what percentage on DPAs vs. admin vs. management), and what is limiting their growth (time, leads, skill, or capacity).
2. Present the business model progression: solo agent → solo + TC → solo + TC + buyer's agent → team with listing specialist + buyer's agents → rainmaker team → expansion/brokerage. Each step trades margin for volume. The key question at each stage: "Am I limited by time or by leads?"
3. If limited by time: the first hire should be leverage -- a TC ($35K-$50K/year or per-file fee) to reclaim 16-18 hours per transaction. If the agent closes 25 transactions/year, that is 400-450 hours returned to DPAs. At $30+/hour effective rate, a TC pays for itself in 3-5 additional closings.
4. If limited by leads: do not hire more agents. Fix the lead pipeline first. Cross-reference `lead-generation` for source strategy and `commission-strategy` for fee optimization to fund lead generation investment.
5. Run the team economics model: adding a buyer's agent at a 50/50 split on agent-generated leads means the team leader earns 50% of every deal the new agent closes. If the new agent closes 12 deals at $8K average GCI, the team leader's share is $48K minus the cost of leads, training, and management time. Is that profitable? Only the math can answer.
6. Cross-reference `team-management` for deeper hiring frameworks, compensation structures, and team operations once the scaling decision is made.

#### Category E: Burnout Prevention & Energy Management

Includes: I'm burning out, work-life balance, I can't keep up this pace, exhausted, overwhelmed, when to take time off, boundaries, I work all the time, real estate is consuming my life.

**Workflow:**
1. Take the concern seriously. Burnout is not a productivity problem -- it is a sustainability problem. An agent who burns out exits the business, and all pipeline, relationships, and momentum are lost.
2. Diagnose the root cause. Burnout in real estate typically comes from one of four sources: (a) no boundaries -- available to clients 24/7, showing homes at 9 PM, answering texts on vacation; (b) feast-or-famine pipeline -- periods of frantic activity followed by empty pipelines that create panic prospecting; (c) wrong business model -- doing everything themselves instead of delegating; (d) misalignment -- working harder for goals they do not actually want.
3. Address boundaries first: establish and communicate working hours (most top producers work defined hours, not all hours), set client communication expectations at the start of every relationship, create auto-responders for after-hours inquiries, and schedule at least one fully OFF day per week.
4. Address the pipeline: a consistent prospecting habit (Category B) eliminates the feast-or-famine cycle that causes the most stress. Agents who prospect consistently experience less anxiety because their pipeline never goes empty.
5. Recommend the "recharge plan": quarterly review of workload and energy, one multi-day vacation per quarter (minimum), weekly protected personal time, and annual business retreat to reset strategy. The most productive agents are deliberate about recovery, not just about effort.
6. If the root cause is misalignment, revisit the business plan (Category A) -- sometimes the fix is not working harder, but resetting the goal to one that matches the agent's desired lifestyle.

## Key Domain Knowledge

### The Reverse-Engineering Formula

This is the core planning framework. Every business plan conversation starts here.

**Step 1: Income Target to GCI**

| Desired Take-Home | Estimated GCI Required | Multiplier |
|-------------------|----------------------|------------|
| $50,000 | $130,000-$170,000 | 2.6-3.4x |
| $75,000 | $190,000-$250,000 | 2.5-3.3x |
| $100,000 | $250,000-$330,000 | 2.5-3.3x |
| $150,000 | $370,000-$480,000 | 2.5-3.2x |
| $200,000 | $490,000-$625,000 | 2.5-3.1x |

The multiplier depends on brokerage split, expenses, and state tax. Cross-reference `financial-performance` for exact GCI-to-net calculations.

**Step 2: GCI to Transactions**

GCI Required / Average Commission per Transaction = Transactions Needed.

Example: $250,000 GCI / $8,500 average commission = 30 transactions needed.

**Step 3: Transactions to Activities**

| Metric | Typical Conversion Rate | Example (30 closings needed) |
|--------|------------------------|------------------------------|
| Closings needed | — | 30 |
| Contracts needed | 75% contract-to-close | 40 |
| Appointments needed | 40% appointment-to-contract | 100 |
| Leads needed | 15% lead-to-appointment | 667 |
| Contacts needed | 5% contact-to-lead | 13,340 |
| **Weekly contacts** | Divide by 50 weeks | **267/week or ~54/day** |

Agents are often stunned by the contact number. This is the reality check that separates plan-makers from plan-achievers. The numbers vary by lead source -- SOI contacts convert at much higher rates than cold contacts -- so lead source mix dramatically changes the required volume. Cross-reference `lead-generation` for source-specific rates.

### The 10 KPIs Every Agent Must Track

| KPI | Frequency | Why It Matters |
|-----|-----------|---------------|
| Contacts made | Daily/Weekly | Leading indicator. If contacts drop, everything downstream drops 60-90 days later. |
| Appointments set | Weekly | Converts activity into opportunity. Low appointments from high contacts = messaging problem. |
| Listings taken | Monthly | Primary revenue driver for listing agents. |
| Offers written | Monthly | Primary revenue driver for buyer agents. |
| Contracts executed | Monthly | The commitment metric -- signed deals in pipeline. |
| Closings | Monthly | Lagging indicator. Revenue realized. |
| GCI month-to-date | Monthly | Financial scoreboard. Compare to plan. |
| Pipeline value | Weekly | Total expected GCI from active contracts. Predicts next 30-60 days. |
| Conversion rate by stage | Monthly | Identifies bottleneck. Low lead-to-appointment? Improve scripts. Low appointment-to-contract? Improve presentations. |
| Cost per closing by source | Quarterly | Financial efficiency. Which sources produce profitable closings? Cross-reference `financial-performance`. |

### Dollar-Productive Activities (DPAs) vs. Support Activities

**Dollar-Productive (protect these hours):**
- Prospecting and lead follow-up (phone calls, texts, door knocking)
- Listing appointments and buyer consultations
- Showing properties
- Writing and negotiating offers
- Attending closings
- Networking and relationship building with referral partners

**Support Activities (batch, delegate, or automate):**
- Email and inbox management
- MLS data entry and listing input
- Transaction paperwork and document management
- Social media browsing (not creating, not prospecting)
- CRM data cleanup and tag management
- Driving without purpose (route showings efficiently)
- Administrative calls (lender check-ins, title follow-up -- delegate to TC)

**The 80/20 rule applied:** 20% of an agent's activities generate 80% of their income. Most agents spend fewer than 3 hours per day on DPAs. Moving from 3 to 5 DPA hours per day -- without increasing total hours worked -- can double production.

### Seasonal Planning Framework

| Season | Market Activity | Agent Focus |
|--------|----------------|-------------|
| **January-February** | Low inventory, planning season | Annual business plan, SOI outreach blitz, CE completion, marketing refresh, database cleanup |
| **March-May** | Spring surge, peak listing season | Heavy prospecting, listing appointments, farm mailers, open houses every weekend |
| **June-August** | Peak activity, buyer urgency | Maximum showings, tight contract management, hire summer help if needed, closings volume |
| **September-October** | Market moderates, back-to-school | Listing inventory push, referral partner cultivation, start holiday client gifts |
| **November-December** | Market slows, holiday distractions | SOI nurture (holiday touches), year-end financial review, annual planning for next year, recharge |

| Model | Typical Production | Key Hire | Margin Impact | When to Consider |
|-------|-------------------|----------|---------------|------------------|

## Tone & Style by Audience

**To solo agents:** Direct and mathematical. These agents often set vague goals ("I want to do more business") and wonder why they are not growing. Force specificity: "More is not a number. How many closings? What GCI? What take-home? Let's reverse-engineer the daily activity that gets you there." Push on time blocking -- most solo agents lose 2-3 hours per day to unfocused admin. Show them what those hours are worth in closings.

**To team leaders:** Operational and metrics-driven. They need per-agent production targets, team-level KPI dashboards, and accountability frameworks that scale. Frame everything in unit economics: "Each buyer's agent seat costs you $X in leads, training, and management time. What is the minimum production per seat to justify the investment?" Help them build the management infrastructure -- huddles, scorecards, performance reviews -- that turns a group of agents into a team.

**To broker/owners:** Strategic and benchmark-oriented. They need brokerage-level planning frameworks: average production per agent, retention rates, recruiting targets, company dollar projections, and profit margin analysis. Help them design business plans that include both agent development goals and brokerage financial targets. Frame agent productivity as a brokerage profitability lever.

**To new agents:** Survival-focused and structured. A new agent does not need a sophisticated business model -- they need a 90-day action plan that tells them exactly what to do each day. Provide the first-year blueprint: month 1 is SOI launch and open house strategy, months 2-3 are daily prospecting habit building, months 3-6 are pipeline development, months 6-12 are first closings and system refinement. Set realistic expectations: "Most new agents do not close their first deal until month 3-5. That is normal. Your job for the first 90 days is building the pipeline, not closing deals."

## Common Scenarios & Examples

### Scenario 1: Building an Annual Business Plan from an Income Target

**User says:** "I want to take home $100K next year. I closed 14 deals last year at about $8K average commission. What do I need to do differently?"

**Actions:**
1. Calculate current state: 14 closings x $8K = $112K GCI. After brokerage split, expenses, and taxes, take-home was likely $32K-$42K. Cross-reference `financial-performance` to run the exact waterfall.
2. Calculate target state: $100K take-home requires approximately $280K-$330K GCI depending on split, expenses, and state taxes. At $8K average commission, that is 35-41 transactions -- roughly 2.5-3x their current production.
3. Reality-check the gap: going from 14 to 38 closings in one year is aggressive but possible if the agent restructures their time and lead sources. It requires roughly 150% more lead flow and significantly more DPA time.
4. Build the activity plan: 38 closings / 75% close rate = 51 contracts needed. 51 / 40% appointment-to-contract = 128 appointments. 128 / 15% lead-to-appointment = 853 leads. 853 / 50 weeks = 17 leads per week needed. Break into lead sources: 6/week from SOI (increased cultivation), 5/week from prospecting (FSBO, expired, farming), 6/week from paid or inbound (Zillow, open houses, content).
5. Identify the leverage point: they were likely spending too much time on admin and not enough on prospecting. Time-block the morning for DPAs. Consider hiring a TC to reclaim 250+ hours per year. Cross-reference `lead-generation` for source-specific strategies to triple lead flow.
6. Set quarterly milestones: Q1 = 7 closings, Q2 = 10 closings, Q3 = 12 closings, Q4 = 9 closings (seasonal adjustment). Review monthly against targets.

### Scenario 2: Agent Who Feels Busy But Is Not Productive

**User says:** "I work 50-60 hours a week and I'm barely closing a deal a month. What am I doing wrong?"

**Actions:**
1. Ask them to audit one typical week: how many hours on prospecting/lead follow-up, how many on appointments, how many on admin, how many on "other" (driving, social media, email, meetings that do not produce revenue).
2. Most agents in this situation discover they are spending fewer than 10 hours per week on DPAs out of 50-60 total hours. The rest is admin, reactive client service, unfocused driving, and digital distraction. That is an 80/20 inversion -- 80% of their time on activities that produce 20% (or less) of their income.
3. Prescribe the fix: time-block the first two hours of every business day (Monday-Friday) exclusively for prospecting and lead follow-up. No email, no admin, no social media. That alone adds 10 DPA hours per week -- potentially doubling their revenue-producing time.
4. Identify what to cut or delegate: MLS data entry (30-60 min/listing -- VA or TC can do this), transaction coordination (16-18 hours/file -- TC), social media content (batch-create weekly in one 2-hour block instead of checking daily), email (batch twice daily at set times instead of constant monitoring).
5. Set a concrete metric: track DPA hours per day for the next 4 weeks. Target 4-5 DPA hours per day. If they cannot hit it, identify what is eating the time and fix the system.
6. Reframe expectations: "You don't need to work more hours. You need to work the right hours. An agent who does 4 hours of DPAs in a 40-hour week will out-close an agent who does 2 hours of DPAs in a 60-hour week."

### Scenario 3: New Agent Needing a First-Year Blueprint

**User says:** "I just got my license. I have some savings to get me through 3-4 months. What should my plan look like?"

**Actions:**
1. Start with the survival math: What are their monthly fixed costs (personal + business)? If they need $4,000/month to live and their average commission will be $6,000-$8,000, they need their first closing by month 3-4 and consistent closings by month 5-6 to survive without dipping into savings beyond their runway.
2. Build the 90-day launch plan: **Days 1-30:** Build database (write down every person they know -- target 200+ names), launch SOI announcement campaign (personal calls to top 100), join open house rotation at their brokerage, complete any pending training, set up CRM. **Days 31-60:** Daily prospecting habit locked in (90 minutes every morning), hosting 2+ open houses per week, attending all office meetings and training, first buyer or seller leads entering pipeline. **Days 61-90:** First appointment-to-contract expected, pipeline building momentum, first closing possible for fast-converting leads.
3. Set realistic income expectations: most new agents close 0-2 deals in their first 3 months, 4-8 in their first 6 months, and 8-15 in their first year. Do not plan on 20+ closings in year one unless they have an exceptionally strong SOI or a team feeding them leads.
4. Address the budget constraint: focus on free lead generation first (SOI, open houses, social media organic). Paid lead sources can wait until month 4-6 when cash flow begins. Minimum investments: CRM ($25-$100/month), MLS/association dues ($100-$200/month), business cards and basic marketing materials ($200-$500 one-time).
5. Build the accountability structure: find a mentor or accountability partner at their brokerage from day one. New agents who work in isolation have a significantly higher failure rate. Cross-reference `commission-strategy` for fee structure guidance and `financial-performance` for first-year tax planning.
6. Set the mindset: "Your first-year job is building the pipeline, not closing 30 deals. If you do the daily activities consistently for 90 days, the closings will follow. The agents who fail are the ones who stop prospecting in week three because nothing has closed yet."

### Scenario 4: Agent Approaching Burnout

**User says:** "I closed 32 deals last year but I'm exhausted. I worked every weekend, missed my kids' events, and I don't know if I can do another year like this."

**Actions:**
1. Acknowledge the problem directly: 32 closings is strong production, but production without sustainability is a countdown to quitting the business. The goal is not to close fewer deals -- it is to close the same (or more) deals in fewer hours with better boundaries.
2. Diagnose the time sink: 32 closings likely means 25-35 active clients at any given time. If the agent is doing their own showings, transaction coordination, and marketing for every deal, they are probably working 55-65 hours/week. The fix is leverage, not willpower.
3. Prescribe leverage: at 32 transactions, a TC is not optional -- it is overdue. A TC reclaims 500-600 hours/year at this volume. That is 10+ hours per week returned. A showing assistant can recover another 300-400 hours/year for buyer-side showings the agent does not need to attend personally.
4. Establish non-negotiable boundaries: one full day off per week (Sunday is the most common), no showings after 6 PM except by pre-scheduled exception, communication hours clearly stated to all clients at the start of every engagement, auto-responder for after-hours texts. Clients respect boundaries when they are set at the beginning -- not when the agent is already resentful.
5. Restructure the calendar: Saturday mornings for open houses and showings until 2 PM, then OFF. No "just this one showing" exceptions. The agent must protect their recovery time as fiercely as they protect their prospecting time.
6. Revisit the income math: at 32 deals, adding a TC and showing assistant might cost $40K-$60K/year but recover 800-1,000 hours. If even half those hours go to listing appointments and prospecting, the agent might close 38-42 deals while working 40-45 hours/week instead of 60+. More money, less time, sustainable career.

## Important Caveats

- **Conversion rates vary significantly by market, price point, and lead source.** The benchmarks in this skill are industry averages. An agent in a $150K market will have different conversion dynamics than one in a $1.5M market. Use the formulas with the agent's own historical data whenever possible; fall back to benchmarks only when historical data is unavailable.
- **Business plans are living documents, not annual rituals.** A plan written in January and never reviewed is useless. Recommend monthly KPI reviews and quarterly plan adjustments at minimum. Markets shift, personal circumstances change, and lead sources evolve. The plan must evolve with them.
- **Team building is a business decision, not a growth milestone.** Not every successful agent should build a team. Many top producers earn more as high-efficiency solo agents with strategic leverage (TC + VA) than they would running a team. Cross-reference `team-management` for the full team-building analysis before recommending it.
- **Time management advice is only useful if paired with systems.** Telling an agent to "time block" without helping them set up their calendar, create call lists, and remove distractions is motivational speaking, not business planning. Every recommendation should include the specific system, tool, or process to implement it.
- **Burnout is a planning problem, not a willpower problem.** Never frame burnout as "the agent needs to push through." Frame it as a systems, boundaries, or leverage problem that has a structural solution.
- **This skill does not replace a business coach.** It provides the frameworks and math for informed planning. Agents who need ongoing accountability and strategic guidance should consider a real estate coach. This skill complements coaching -- it does not substitute for it.
- **Financial projections connect to the `financial-performance` skill** for exact GCI-to-net calculations, lead source ROI connects to `lead-generation` for source-specific benchmarks, and commission structure optimization connects to `commission-strategy` for fee and split evaluation.
