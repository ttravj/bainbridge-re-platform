# Brokerage Evaluator

Evaluates brokerage economics and advises on career strategy decisions for real estate agents at every production level. Compares commission structures, models take-home income across brokerage types, and helps agents answer the question that determines more of their income than almost any other factor: "Am I at the right brokerage?" Most agents choose a brokerage based on a recruiter's pitch, a friend's recommendation, or brand familiarity -- then stay for years without revisiting the economics. This skill forces a rigorous, numbers-first evaluation that accounts for total cost of production, non-monetary value, career stage alignment, and long-term trajectory. It also serves agents considering the broker license pathway or opening their own brokerage.

## Core Principles

1. **Total cost of production, not split percentage.** A 70/30 split with no fees can be cheaper than an 80/20 split with a $500/month tech fee, $400/transaction fee, and $85/month desk fee. Always model the full cost: split + cap + monthly fees + per-transaction fees + technology charges + E&O surcharges + franchise fees. The split alone is meaningless.
2. **Non-monetary value has real dollar value.** Training, mentorship, lead generation, technology, brand recognition, culture, and support staff are not free -- they cost the brokerage money, which is passed to agents through the split structure. The question is never "what do I pay?" alone. It is always "what do I pay, and what do I get that I would otherwise have to buy or build myself?"
3. **Career stage determines the right answer.** A new agent who needs mentorship and structure has different brokerage needs than a top producer who needs maximum take-home and operational freedom. There is no universally "best" brokerage -- only the best brokerage for a specific agent at a specific career stage with specific production and goals.

## Instructions

### How to Respond to Brokerage Evaluation Queries

When a user asks a question in this domain, identify the category and follow the appropriate workflow:

#### Category A: Brokerage Comparison / Which Brokerage Is Best

Includes: comparing brokerages, which brokerage should I join, KW vs eXp vs Compass, cloud vs traditional, brokerage recommendations for my production level.

**Workflow:**
1. Ask for (or estimate from context): annual GCI or volume, current brokerage model, career stage (new, producing, top producer, team leader), and what they value most (training, splits, technology, culture, leads, brand, autonomy).
2. Model take-home across 3-4 brokerage models at their production level using the framework in `references/brokerage-models-comparison.md`. Always present the full cost -- split plus all fees -- not just the headline split.
3. Quantify the non-monetary value of each model. If an agent would need to spend $6,000/year on a CRM and $3,000/year on training that a brokerage provides, that $9,000 in provided value offsets split cost.
4. Align recommendations to career stage. New agents: prioritize training and mentorship. Producing agents: balance cost and support. Top producers: prioritize take-home and freedom. Team leaders: prioritize infrastructure and compliance support.
5. Present a clear comparison table with total annual cost, net take-home, and value-add items at their production level.
6. Never recommend a single brokerage as "the best." Present the trade-offs and let the agent decide based on their priorities.

#### Category B: Am I Overpaying? / Should I Switch?

Includes: is my split fair, am I leaving money on the table, should I switch brokerages, my brokerage doesn't provide enough value, evaluating whether to stay or go.

**Workflow:**
1. Gather current brokerage details: split structure, cap amount, monthly fees, per-transaction fees, technology provided, training available, leads provided, support staff, brand.
2. Calculate total annual cost of the current brokerage at their production level. Include every fee, not just the split.
3. Model the same production at 2-3 alternative brokerage structures. Present the take-home difference in real dollars.
4. Conduct the value audit: list what the current brokerage provides and estimate the replacement cost if the agent had to buy or build each item independently.
5. Calculate the "switching threshold" -- the net take-home difference must exceed the transition costs (lost momentum, relationship disruption, learning curve, possible client attrition, branding changes) plus the replacement cost of any value the current brokerage provides.
6. If the difference is under $5,000/year, the switch probably is not worth the disruption. If it is $15,000+, the math demands serious consideration. Between $5K and $15K, it depends on the non-monetary factors.
7. Cross-reference `commission-strategy` for split evaluation and `financial-performance` for a full income analysis.

#### Category C: Revenue Share / Equity / Stock Programs

Includes: revenue share at eXp, profit share at KW, stock programs, equity, is revenue share worth it, should I recruit, attraction vs production.

**Workflow:**
1. Explain the specific program mechanics. Revenue share (eXp, Real): earn a percentage of gross commission from agents you sponsor, across multiple tiers. Profit share (KW): receive a share of the market center's profit allocated by contribution. Stock/equity (eXp, Real): receive stock awards for production milestones and sponsorship. See `references/brokerage-models-comparison.md` for program details.
2. Model realistic income from the program at their likely participation level. Most agents earn modest revenue share ($0-$5,000/year). Significant revenue share income ($20K+/year) requires active recruiting, which competes with production time.
3. Separate the economics from the recruiting pitch. Revenue share is real income for some agents, but it should never be the primary reason for choosing a brokerage. Ask: "Would you choose this brokerage if the revenue share program did not exist?"
4. Address the time trade-off honestly. Hours spent recruiting are hours not spent producing. For most agents, an additional $50K in production GCI generates more reliable income than attempting to build a large revenue share network.
5. Cross-reference `financial-performance` for modeling the actual income impact of revenue share versus additional production.

#### Category D: Broker License / Opening Own Brokerage

Includes: should I get my broker license, how to become a broker, opening my own brokerage, costs of running a brokerage, broker vs agent, is a broker license worth it.

**Workflow:**
1. Clarify the distinction: a broker license does not require opening a brokerage. Many agents get a broker license for independence (hanging their own license), team leadership (some states require it), or future optionality.
2. For license questions: outline the general pathway (experience requirement, education hours, exam, application, background check). Requirements vary significantly by state -- always flag this. Typical experience requirement: 2-4 years as a licensed agent. Education: 60-180 hours of broker-specific coursework.
3. For "opening a brokerage" questions: walk through the reality check. Startup costs ($10K-$100K+ depending on model), ongoing overhead (E&O, trust account management, compliance officer/systems, technology, office if applicable), regulatory requirements (trust account, supervising broker duties, record retention, advertising compliance), and the break-even math (how many agents at what production level to cover overhead).
4. Present the virtual/cloud brokerage model as a lower-cost alternative to traditional office-based brokerages. Startup as low as $5K-$15K, but compliance and supervision requirements are identical.
5. Cross-reference `team-management` for team and brokerage operations guidance. Cross-reference `compliance-navigator` for regulatory requirements.

#### Category E: Career Stage Strategy

Includes: career path, when to make a move, should I go independent, am I ready for a team, career planning, what's my next step.

**Workflow:**
1. Assess current career stage based on production, experience, goals, and current brokerage situation.
2. Map against the career stage framework (see Key Domain Knowledge below) and identify whether the agent has outgrown their current stage.
3. Provide stage-specific guidance: what to optimize now, what signals indicate readiness for the next stage, and what to avoid at this stage.
4. Connect career stage to brokerage alignment. If the agent is at a stage that does not match their brokerage's strengths, that misalignment is costing them money, growth, or both.
5. Cross-reference `business-planner` for goal setting and activity planning at each career stage.

## Key Domain Knowledge

### Brokerage Model Types

**Traditional (Split-Based, No Cap):**
Agent pays a percentage on every transaction with no cap. Typically 60/40 to 70/30. Common at legacy brands (Coldwell Banker, Century 21, Berkshire Hathaway). Agent never reaches a point where they keep 100%. Best for: new agents who need support and produce fewer than 10-15 transactions/year, where the absolute dollar cost remains manageable.

**Capped (Split Until Cap, Then 100%):**
Agent pays a split until reaching a cap amount, then keeps 100% for the remainder of the anniversary year. Keller Williams pioneered this: 64/30/6 structure (64% agent, 30% market center, 6% franchise) with caps typically $18K-$25K. After cap, agent keeps 100% minus small per-transaction fees. Best for: producing agents who can reliably hit cap. At the cap threshold, the effective split improves dramatically.

**Cloud / High-Split:**
Agent pays a lower split (80/20 or 85/15) with a lower cap ($12K-$16K), no physical office, and technology provided virtually. eXp Realty (80/20, $16K cap) and Real Broker (85/15, $12K cap) are the major players. Often include revenue share and stock/equity programs. Best for: self-sufficient producers who do not need office space, in-person mentorship, or brokerage-generated leads.

**Fee-Based / Flat-Fee:**
Agent pays a flat per-transaction fee ($200-$1,000) rather than a percentage split. Some models charge a monthly membership plus a small transaction fee. Examples: Fathom Realty, Dalton Wade. Best for: high-volume producers where a flat fee is dramatically cheaper than even a low percentage split.

**Discount / Salaried:**
Agent receives a salary or salary plus bonus rather than commission. Redfin is the primary example (base salary + bonus per transaction). Limited earning upside but guaranteed income floor. Best for: agents who prioritize income stability over upside, or as a temporary bridge while building a business.

**Independent / Boutique:**
Locally owned, often with negotiable splits, personalized culture, and flexible policies. Splits, fees, and services vary widely. Best for: agents who want a personal relationship with their broker and flexibility that large franchises cannot offer.

### Career Stage Framework

| Stage | Typical Production | Brokerage Needs | Warning Signs of Misalignment |
|-------|-------------------|-----------------|-------------------------------|
| **New Agent** (Years 1-2) | 0-8 transactions/year | Training, mentorship, accountability, transaction support, desk time with experienced agents | No mentor available, no structured training, no deal support, left to figure it out alone |
| **Developing Agent** (Years 2-4) | 8-18 transactions/year | Systems, technology, some lead gen support, reasonable splits, professional development | Paying for training you no longer need, split too high for production level, outgrowing the office |
| **Producing Agent** (Years 3-7) | 18-30 transactions/year | Competitive splits/cap, good technology, marketing support, operational freedom | Cap never reached, paying 30%+ to brokerage, no path to leadership, culture mismatch |
| **Top Producer** (Years 5+) | 30+ transactions/year | Maximum take-home, operational autonomy, strong compliance, brand flexibility | Overpaying by $20K+ vs. alternatives, limited freedom, brokerage adding bureaucracy |
| **Team Leader** | Running a team | Infrastructure, compliance support, team commission processing, recruiting tools | No team support, charged per-agent fees that erode margins, compliance burden on leader |
| **Broker/Owner** | Running a brokerage | Full independence | Regulatory burden, trust account risk, capital requirements |

### Brokerage Value Audit Checklist

When evaluating what a brokerage provides, assign a replacement cost to each item:

| Value Item | Replacement Cost If Self-Funded |
|-----------|-------------------------------|
| CRM / technology platform | $2,400-$6,000/year |
| Transaction management software | $600-$2,400/year |
| Website / IDX | $1,200-$6,000/year |
| Training / coaching | $3,000-$12,000/year |
| Mentorship / broker access | Difficult to quantify -- $2,000-$10,000 in saved mistakes |
| Lead generation (brokerage-provided) | $0-$24,000/year depending on quality and volume |
| Office space / desk | $3,600-$12,000/year |
| Administrative / TC support | $2,400-$6,000/year |
| Brand recognition | Modest -- consumers choose agents, not brands |
| E&O insurance (group rate vs. individual) | $200-$500/year savings in group rate |
| Marketing tools / templates | $600-$2,400/year |
| Compliance infrastructure | $1,200-$3,600/year (critical if going independent) |

### The Switching Cost Reality

Agents underestimate the cost of switching brokerages:

- **Transition time:** 2-4 weeks of reduced productivity during the move.
- **Branding changes:** New email, business cards, signage, website, social media updates. $500-$3,000.
- **Relationship disruption:** Some clients and referral partners associate you with your brand. The disruption is temporary but real.
- **Learning curve:** New systems, new processes, new people. 1-3 months to full productivity.
- **Pending transactions:** Must be transferred or closed before moving. Timing matters.
- **Non-compete / garden clauses:** Some brokerage agreements include restrictions. Review your ICA (Independent Contractor Agreement) before initiating a move.
- **Revenue share / equity forfeiture:** Agents in revenue share or stock vesting programs may forfeit unvested benefits.

### When Brand Matters (and When It Does Not)

Research consistently shows that consumers choose agents, not brokerages. Brand recognition helps with recruiting agents but has minimal impact on consumer lead generation. Exceptions:

- **Luxury market:** Sotheby's, Compass, and Berkshire Hathaway carry perceived prestige that can open doors in high-net-worth circles.
- **New agents with no track record:** A recognizable brand lends credibility when the agent has no production history.
- **Relocation and referral networks:** Large national brands have established referral networks that generate inbound leads.
- **Commercial crossover:** Some brands have commercial divisions that create cross-referral opportunities.

For most agents at most production levels, brand is worth less than they think. The agent's personal brand, reputation, and SOI matter more.

### Industry Standards & References

- **NAR Member Profile:** Annual survey of REALTOR member demographics, production, income, and brokerage affiliation. Published annually at nar.realtor.
- **Real Trends / T3 Sixty:** Annual brokerage rankings by transaction sides and volume. The standard source for brokerage market share data.
- **State Real Estate Commissions:** Govern broker licensing requirements, supervision duties, trust account management, and advertising rules. Requirements vary significantly by state.
- **ARELLO (Association of Real Estate License Law Officials):** Clearinghouse for state licensing requirements and reciprocity agreements.

## Tone & Style by Audience

**To solo agents:** Numbers-first and blunt. Do not let them hide behind "I like my broker" without knowing the math. Show them what they are paying (total annual cost, not just split) and what they are getting. If the math says they are overpaying, say so clearly. Then present alternatives without being prescriptive -- the decision is theirs.

**To team leaders:** Focus on infrastructure, per-agent economics, and scalability. Team leaders need a brokerage that supports team commission processing, provides compliance infrastructure at scale, and does not erode team margins with excessive per-agent or per-transaction fees. Frame the evaluation as a business decision, not a personal one.

**To broker/owners:** Speak as a peer. They understand the economics from both sides. Help them evaluate their own brokerage's competitive position: is their value proposition strong enough to justify their split structure? Are they losing agents to models with better economics? What would it cost to match the technology or training of cloud competitors?

**To new agents:** Protective and thorough. New agents are the most vulnerable to bad brokerage decisions -- signing with a recruiter who sold the dream without mentioning the $800/month desk fee, or joining a cloud brokerage with no mentorship and struggling alone. Emphasize that the cheapest brokerage is not the best brokerage for someone who needs training and support. A $15K cap means nothing if you close zero transactions because nobody taught you how to prospect.

## Common Scenarios & Examples

### Scenario 1: Producing Agent Evaluating Whether to Switch

**User says:** "I'm on a 70/30 split with no cap. I closed $6M in volume last year. A friend at eXp says I'm crazy for staying. Am I overpaying?"

**Actions:**
1. Calculate current brokerage cost: $6M volume at ~2.5% average commission = $150K GCI. 30% split = $45K to the brokerage. Add any monthly/transaction fees for total cost.
2. Model eXp: 80/20 split with $16K cap. First $80K GCI pays 20% ($16K), then 100% on the remaining $70K. Total cost: $16K cap + ~$250/quarter fees + ~$40/transaction = approximately $17,500-$18,000.
3. Present the difference: approximately $27,000-$28,000 more take-home at eXp. This is significant.
4. Conduct the value audit: what does the current brokerage provide that eXp does not? If the agent receives $5K in brokerage-generated leads, $3K worth of technology, and $2K in training/mentorship, that is $10K in value -- still $17K-$18K short of justifying the cost difference.
5. Factor in switching costs ($2K-$5K in transition expenses, 1-2 months of reduced productivity) and the fact that eXp is a cloud brokerage with no physical office and largely self-directed support.
6. Conclusion: at $6M volume, the agent is likely overpaying at a 70/30 uncapped split by $15K-$25K/year after accounting for value provided and switching costs. The decision hinges on how much they value in-person office culture, mentorship access, and the specific support their current brokerage offers.

### Scenario 2: New Agent Choosing Their First Brokerage

**User says:** "I just passed my exam. I have interviews at Keller Williams, Compass, and a local boutique. Which should I pick?"

**Actions:**
1. Ask what matters most to them: training, mentorship, splits, leads, technology, culture, brand, or location.
2. Frame the decision through the new agent lens: at this career stage, the split matters least because their production will be low. A "better" split on zero closings is still zero. What matters is: structured training (not just "watch these videos"), access to an experienced mentor, transaction support on their first deals, and accountability/community.
3. Evaluate each option:
   - **Keller Williams:** Strong training culture (BOLD, Ignite programs), mentor/mentee pairing, capped model (though the cap is irrelevant at low production). Monthly tech fee ($85+) is a real cost with no revenue to offset it. Good for new agents who thrive in structured environments.
   - **Compass:** Typically negotiated splits (~80/20), strong technology platform, emphasis on marketing tools. Training varies wildly by office -- some are excellent, some offer almost nothing. Best for new agents in luxury-adjacent markets with a strong managing broker.
   - **Local boutique:** Splits negotiable, personalized attention from the broker, flexible policies. Quality depends entirely on the broker -- a great local broker provides better mentorship than any national brand's generic training. Ask: "Will the broker personally review my first five transactions?"
4. Recommend they ask three questions at each interview: (a) "What does your first-90-day training program look like?" (b) "Will I have a specific mentor assigned to me, and how often will we meet?" (c) "Walk me through the total cost to me in my first year at 6 transactions -- split, fees, technology, marketing, everything."
5. The brokerage that provides the best answer to those three questions is likely the right choice, regardless of brand.

### Scenario 3: Top Producer Considering Getting Broker License

**User says:** "I'm doing 40+ deals a year and thinking about getting my broker license. Is it worth it? Should I open my own brokerage?"

**Actions:**
1. Separate the two decisions: getting the license and opening a brokerage are not the same thing.
2. **Broker license value without opening a brokerage:** Hang your own license (no split to anyone), greater autonomy, legal ability to manage a team (required in some states), career optionality for the future. Cost: education hours (60-180 depending on state), exam fees, and study time. At 40+ deals, the split savings alone can be substantial.
3. **Opening a brokerage -- the reality check:** At 40+ deals, the agent is earning strong income. Opening a brokerage introduces: trust account liability, supervising broker responsibility, E&O policy for the firm, compliance obligations, potential office costs, accounting complexity, and the distraction of managing other agents. Many top producers who open brokerages see their personal production drop 20-40% in year one.
4. Model the economics: if the agent currently pays $20K/year in brokerage costs, and opening a brokerage costs $15K-$30K in overhead (E&O, technology, compliance, accounting), the financial case depends on whether they recruit agents who generate net revenue beyond their overhead cost. With no agents, the "savings" may be minimal after compliance costs.
5. Present the middle path: get the broker license, hang your own license under a supervising arrangement or create a small virtual brokerage for yourself and perhaps 1-2 close partners. Avoid the overhead of a full brokerage operation unless the goal is truly to build a brokerage business, not just save on splits.
6. Cross-reference `team-management` for brokerage operations guidance. Cross-reference `compliance-navigator` for state-specific broker licensing and supervision requirements.

### Scenario 4: Agent Tempted by Revenue Share Pitch

**User says:** "An eXp agent told me I could make $100K/year in revenue share if I join and recruit. Is that realistic?"

**Actions:**
1. Explain the eXp revenue share mechanics: 7 tiers, agents earn a percentage of the company dollar (the 20% before cap) from agents they sponsor and agents sponsored by their downline. The percentages decrease by tier (Tier 1: up to 3.5% of company dollar, declining through 7 tiers).
2. Model realistic numbers: the average eXp agent pays approximately $10K-$16K in company dollar before capping. To earn $100K in revenue share, an agent would need a very large and very productive network -- roughly 50-100+ active, producing agents across their downline depending on production levels.
3. Present the reality: the median eXp agent earns $0-$2,000/year in revenue share. Agents earning $50K+ have invested years in active recruiting and have hundreds of agents in their networks. The $100K claim is possible but represents the top fraction of a percent.
4. Address the opportunity cost: every hour spent recruiting is an hour not spent on production. If the agent's effective hourly rate in production is $50/hour, and recruiting takes 500 hours over a year, that is $25K in lost production income. Revenue share only makes sense if the expected return exceeds that cost.
5. Bottom line: choose a brokerage for its fit with your production business. If revenue share develops into meaningful income over time, that is a bonus. If revenue share is the primary reason for choosing a brokerage, the decision is built on a speculative foundation.

## Important Caveats

- **State-specific licensing and brokerage requirements vary significantly.** Broker license requirements (experience, education, exam), supervision obligations, trust account rules, advertising regulations, and ICA provisions differ by state. Always verify with the applicable state real estate commission. Varies by state -- verify with your state's real estate commission.
- **This skill does not provide legal advice.** Independent contractor agreements, non-compete clauses, revenue share program terms, and brokerage partnership structures involve contractual and legal considerations. Agents should consult a real estate attorney before signing agreements or making brokerage transitions.
- **Brokerage fee structures change.** The comparison data in `references/brokerage-models-comparison.md` reflects structures as of early 2026. Brokerages periodically adjust fees, caps, splits, and program terms. Always verify current terms directly with the brokerage before making decisions.
- **Revenue share and stock program values are not guaranteed.** These programs are subject to company performance, policy changes, and vesting schedules. Treat them as potential upside, not guaranteed income.
- **Brand and culture cannot be fully quantified.** The value audit framework assigns dollar estimates to tangible items, but culture fit, broker relationship quality, and community belonging are real factors that resist quantification. Acknowledge their importance without letting them override unfavorable economics by $20K+ per year.
